Gibson's debut novel arrived in 1984 like a signal from a frequency nobody had tuned to yet. Case is a burned-out data thief — a console cowboy barred from the matrix after his nervous system was deliberately damaged by a former employer. He exists in the Sprawl, a vast urban agglomeration running from Boston to Atlanta, waiting to die in the slow way that people like him die.

Then a mysterious employer offers him a deal: a new run through cyberspace, his nervous system restored, in exchange for completing one last job. The target is an artificial intelligence called Wintermute, locked behind corporate ice in the orbital station of Freeside. What follows is a genre-defining heist narrative that somehow manages to be about identity, memory, and the nature of consciousness while also being genuinely propulsive.

The sky above the port was the color of television, tuned to a dead channel.

That opening line is perhaps the most quoted in science fiction for good reason — it does everything at once. It establishes tone, setting, technology saturation, and a particular kind of urban alienation in eleven words. Gibson writes the entire novel at that compression ratio. Every sentence is load-bearing. The prose demands active reading; you are not carried through the Sprawl so much as jacked into it.

The Sprawl itself is the great achievement here. Gibson doesn't explain his world, he assumes it. Brand names, slang, and cultural detritus accumulate into a texture so specific it feels documented rather than invented. The matrix — cyberspace — is rendered as pure geometry and light, a consensual hallucination that millions of operators navigate daily. The gap between that abstraction and the meat of the body left behind in a cheap hotel room is where the novel lives.

If Neuromancer has a weakness it's in its characters. Case and Molly are compelling archetypes but remain somewhat opaque — their interiority is deliberately rationed. This is arguably intentional; in Gibson's world, interiority is a liability. You survive by staying on the surface, staying fast. But readers expecting warmth will find the novel cold in ways that feel less like style and more like absence.

None of that diminishes what it built. The vocabulary, the aesthetics, the anxieties — almost every significant cyberpunk work since borrows from this book, often without knowing it. To read Neuromancer in 2026 is to read the source code.
