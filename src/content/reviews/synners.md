Pat Cadigan is systematically under-discussed in cyberpunk retrospectives dominated by Gibson and Stephenson, and Synners is the clearest argument for correcting that. **The novel is about direct neural interface — sockets that allow video artists to jack in and create content from inside their nervous systems** — and it is more interested in what that technology does to human bodies than in what it allows those bodies to accomplish.

The central cast is a group of video artists and musicians working in an industry that is transforming so rapidly that the ground is literally shifting. Gina and Visual Mark are the creative heart of the book, their relationship strained by Mark's increasing integration with the network and decreasing presence in physical space. The Cass and Gabe subplot involves a computer virus that doesn't target machines — it targets sockets, and through sockets, the people wearing them.

> The interface was the new skin. The virus didn't want your data. It wanted your nervous system.

Cadigan's prose is less stylized than Gibson's, more interested in character interiority and less in surface texture. This is a feature, not a limitation. The characters in Synners are genuinely inhabited. You understand why they make the choices they make, including the catastrophically bad ones.

**The body horror in Synners is unusually specific.** The virus storyline requires the novel to describe neurological damage in precise physiological terms, and Cadigan does it without flinching. The scenes in which characters experience seizures as network events — as something happening simultaneously in flesh and data — are among the most disturbing in the genre.

Synners won the 1991 Arthur C. Clarke Award and has been largely absent from popular discussion ever since, which is one of the genre's recurring failures. It belongs on the same shelf as Neuromancer.
