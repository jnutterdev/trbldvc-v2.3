Serial Experiments Lain is not about the internet. It is about what happens to a self when the boundary between internal experience and networked reality becomes permeable. **Lain Iwakura is thirteen years old and dissolving.** The dissolution is not dramatic. It accumulates quietly, episode by episode, until you are watching something that resembles the teenager from episode one the way a photograph resembles a person.

The series was produced by Triangle Staff in 1998, the year Google was founded, the year that networked life was becoming ordinary. Its prescience lies not in predicting specific technologies but in anticipating a psychological condition: the sense that identity is constructed through network presence, that the self you perform online and the self that sits in a room are not the same entity, and that the relationship between them is unstable.

> Present day. Present time.

The animation style is deliberately alienating — static backgrounds rendered with obsessive detail, characters that barely move, power lines used as a visual motif to the point of iconography. Yoshitoshi ABe's character designs give Lain enormous eyes and a body language of permanent uncertainty. **She is uncomfortable to look at because she is uncomfortable being looked at.**

The sound design by Yoshihiro Ike is one of the series' great achievements. Silence is deployed as an instrument. When sound enters, it is often wrong — mechanical hum, distorted voices, the ambient noise of electronics rather than music. The effect is a sustained sense of low-level wrongness that never resolves.

Serial Experiments Lain does not explain itself. The mythology accumulates but never consolidates into a clean thesis. This is either its central flaw or its deepest virtue, depending on what you want from the experience. What it offers is something rare: a work that gets more interesting as the cultural context around it changes, because the questions it asks are not historical.
