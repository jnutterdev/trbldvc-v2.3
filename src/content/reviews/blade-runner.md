There is a version of Blade Runner criticism that focuses on its narrative shortcomings — the clumsy voiceover in the theatrical cut, the underdeveloped female characters, the ending that was altered several times across as many releases. That criticism is not wrong. It is also almost entirely beside the point.

**Blade Runner is a work of pure cinema.** The question it asks — what makes something real, what makes something human — is delivered primarily through image, not dialogue. Jordan Cronenweth's photography turns Los Angeles 2019 into a place that feels both overcrowded and abandoned, perpetually wet, lit by acid neon and the searchlights of police spinners moving through perpetual smog.

> More human than human.

Vangelis's score is one of the few film soundtracks that constitutes a complete artistic statement on its own terms. The synthesizer textures — warm, decaying, slightly off — do something unusual: they make the film's android characters feel more melancholy than the humans. The score mourns on their behalf.

Harrison Ford's Deckard is often cited as the film's weakness, and it's true that the character is deliberately blunted. But Rutger Hauer's Roy Batty is one of cinema's great antagonists — a creature trying to extend a life he knows is ending, whose final speech remains the most eloquent four lines ever written for a science fiction film.

The replicants are sympathetic. The humans are compromised. The moral architecture is deliberately inverted, and Scott never resolves it cleanly. **That irresolution is the point.** The film asks whether empathy is biological or performative, and then refuses to answer.

Almost every significant visual work in the cyberpunk genre — every rain-soaked corridor, every neon-reflected puddle, every dystopian skyline — borrows from what Scott and Cronenweth built here. Blade Runner is not a film about the future. It is the future's aesthetic foundation.
