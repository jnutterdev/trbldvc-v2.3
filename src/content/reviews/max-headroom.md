The world of Max Headroom is twenty minutes into the future. That's the actual number given in the show's premise, and it turns out to be more prescient than anyone involved probably intended. **The dystopia is not nuclear or ecological — it's mediaological.** The corporations that run this world run it through television ratings, and the show's central villain is not a dictator but a network executive.

Max Headroom the character — an AI newscaster who is the digitized consciousness of journalist Edison Carter, running on corporate hardware — is one of the more interesting constructs in the genre. He is not a threat. He is a disruption. He glitches, stutters, finishes his own sentences incorrectly, and occasionally broadcasts things the corporations would prefer remain hidden. His power is his inability to be fully controlled.

> The truth is out there — assuming it isn't owned by someone.

The show ran for two seasons on ABC in 1987-88, which is remarkable. Network television in the late eighties was not an environment hospitable to stories about how television corporations manipulate public consciousness. The writers pushed into that territory regularly and managed to keep the show on air long enough to develop some genuinely unsettling episodes.

**The production design is exceptional given the budget.** The set designers understood that cyberpunk aesthetics are built from surplus and repurposing — screens everywhere, cabling as decoration, corporate logos layered over corporate logos. The world feels used.

Where the show falters is in its episodic structure, which was mandated by network conventions the premise was simultaneously critiquing. The monster-of-the-week format undercuts the cumulative world-building that the best cyberpunk requires. But within those constraints, Max Headroom managed more genuine subversion than most shows with ten times the creative freedom.
