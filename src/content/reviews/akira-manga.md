The 1988 film adaptation of Akira is one of the great achievements in animated cinema. **The manga that preceded it by six years is something else entirely.** Otomo spent from 1982 to 1990 drawing the full extent of Neo-Tokyo's destruction across roughly two thousand pages, and the scope of what he produced makes the film look like a highlight reel.

This is not a criticism of the film. The film is exceptional. It is a statement about the manga's ambition. Characters who exist only as names in the film become fully realized across hundreds of pages. The political infrastructure of a post-disaster Tokyo — the resistance factions, the military government, the religious movements that form around Akira — is given room to breathe and contradict itself.

> The city was not built for this. Nothing is built for this.

Otomo's draftsmanship is among the finest in comics history. He draws machinery with a technical precision that makes it look functional, crowds with a documentary density that makes them feel inevitable, destruction with a weight that makes you feel the mass of what is falling. **The crosshatching on his cityscapes is a form of architectural rendering, not illustration.**

The manga's greatest achievement over the film is its treatment of Tetsuo. In the film, he is a villain — a psychic weapon that needs to be contained. In the manga, he is a deeply damaged adolescent granted power without context or support, and Otomo is meticulous about charting the psychology of that damage. His arc is genuinely tragic in ways the film cannot accommodate.

For anyone who loves the film, the manga is not supplementary. It is the primary text — the full statement of which the film is a fragment.
