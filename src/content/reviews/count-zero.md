Count Zero follows Neuromancer with a structural ambition that the first novel's focused momentum didn't require: **three separate storylines running simultaneously**, converging on the same mystery from different angles. Bobby Newmark, Turner, and Marly Krushkhova don't know they are in the same story. The reader does.

The mystery concerns boxes — shadow boxes, in the Joseph Cornell tradition, appearing in the matrix with no clear origin. The Wintermute/Neuromancer event from the first novel has changed something in cyberspace, and the entities that emerge from that change are speaking in the language of Haitian voodoo. Gibson grafts Caribbean spiritualism onto corporate technoculture with a lightness that should not work and does.

> The matrix is God. The matrix is everything. The loa ride in the net now.

Turner is the closest Count Zero comes to a conventional action hero, and Gibson works against type by making his storyline the most emotionally resonant of the three. The Marly plot — an art dealer investigating corporate patronage of AI creativity — is the most formally interesting, and the Bobby plot is the most straightforwardly cyberpunk.

**The convergence in the final act is not entirely satisfying.** Gibson pulls the three threads together with less elegance than he might have, and the climax is shorter and more elliptical than the setup earns. But the journey is exceptional.

What Count Zero does better than Neuromancer is texture. The Sprawl feels more inhabited, the corporate infrastructure more detailed, the class dynamics more explicit. Gibson is expanding his world rather than just populating it, and the expansion is genuinely interesting.
