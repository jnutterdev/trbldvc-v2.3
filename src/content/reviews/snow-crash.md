Snow Crash opens with a three-page sequence detailing the logistics of pizza delivery in near-future Los Angeles with the intensity of a technical manual. **Hiro Protagonist — the name is not a joke, or rather, the joke is the point** — is the best pizza deliveryman in the world and also one of the best sword fighters and also the cowriter of the Black Sun, the coolest club in the Metaverse. Stephenson piles these credentials on without irony, and somehow it works.

The novel that coined "Metaverse" as a cultural term remains one of the strangest objects in the cyberpunk canon. It is simultaneously a satire of the genre it participates in, a genuinely rigorous piece of near-future speculation, a thriller about ancient Sumerian linguistics, and an extended joke about California privatization. All of these things are happening at once, at velocity.

> The Deliverator belongs to an elite order, a hallowed subcategory.

Stephenson's worldbuilding is more systematically imagined than Gibson's. Where Neuromancer builds texture through implication, Snow Crash constructs infrastructure — the franchise model of future America, the physics of the Metaverse, the economics of information in a post-nation-state world. It is a novel that clearly enjoyed its own research.

The Y.T. plotlines are often stronger than Hiro's, which is part of what makes the novel feel uneven. The Sumerian linguistics subplot is both its most ambitious element and the source of its longest stretches of exposition. **The pacing lurches** in ways that Gibson's never does.

None of that prevents Snow Crash from being essential. It predicted the Metaverse — not just the word but the business logic — with a precision that should have been embarrassing to Silicon Valley investors who claimed to have invented it. The jokes are funnier than most cyberpunk manages, and the action sequences are genuinely kinetic. It earns its reputation.
