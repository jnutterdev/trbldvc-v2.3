Nemesis, also released in 1992, is the first movie in a series of sequels. This film is a cyberpunk action film set in the near future, where criminals enhance themselves with cybernetic components, making them "more than human", while illegal androids have also become commonplace. Olivier Gruner (Lionheart) stars in the film as a cybernetically enhanced bounty hunter who is on the run from a militant group, led by Tim Thomerson (Trancers), where he must survive.

**The film's debts are obvious and unpaid.** Robocop's cyborg body horror, Terminator's relentless pursuit structure, Blade Runner's android identity politics — Nemesis borrows from all of them without the budget to match. Albert Pyun is not a subtle filmmaker. The action is blunt, the plotting is functional, and the performances range from committed to mechanical.

What it does have is energy. Pyun shoots action with a kinetic roughness that higher-budget productions often sand away, and Gruner carries the physical demands of the role convincingly. The world it sketches — cybernetic criminals, black market augmentation, institutional paranoia — is a legitimate cyberpunk setting even if the execution is uneven.

> More machine than man, more man than machine.

What I appreciate of the movie is that while it does draw from previous films like Robocop, Blade Runner, and Terminator, it's original enough to stand out on its own as a hard-boiled cyberpunk film. I did enjoy it overall, but I wouldn't say that this is my favorite movie. However, it is a good introduction into the genre.

The sequels take the premise in increasingly strange directions with diminishing budgets. The original Nemesis is where the concept is at its most coherent, and for what it is — a low-budget cyberpunk action film from the early nineties — it delivers.
