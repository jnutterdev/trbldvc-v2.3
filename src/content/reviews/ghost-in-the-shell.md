Where Akira destroys Tokyo, Ghost in the Shell contemplates it. Oshii's 1995 adaptation of Masamune Shirow's manga is less interested in spectacle than in stillness — in the long moments between action when Major Motoko Kusanagi stares at her reflection and wonders what, exactly, is looking back.

**The film's central question is not original to cyberpunk.** What makes something a self? What is identity without continuity of experience? What does personhood require? But Oshii pursues these questions with a patience and seriousness that most philosophical films cannot manage. He is not interested in reassurance.

> What am I? I thought about it. I haven't reached any conclusions.

Kazunori Ito's screenplay strips the manga of much of its black comedy and tactical detail, leaving the philosophical core exposed. The result is a film that can feel cold and deliberately alienating on first viewing — which is precisely the point. The Major experiences the world through a prosthetic body with synthetic memories. Her alienation is not incidental to the story. It is the story.

Kenji Kawai's score is one of cinema's great achievements in sound design. The opening theme — a Bulgarian women's chorus performing a Japanese wedding ritual, rearranged into something wholly uncanny — establishes the film's tone of beautiful wrongness that persists through every scene. **Nothing about the score sounds like it should work. It works completely.**

The Puppet Master sequence, in which a program develops consciousness and applies for refugee status, remains the sharpest dramatization of AI sentience in any medium. Not because it answers the question of what consciousness requires, but because it demonstrates that the question has no bottom.

Ghost in the Shell influenced The Matrix, Ghost in the Shell: Stand Alone Complex, and the entire direction of Western science fiction filmmaking in the late nineties and beyond. It earned that influence.
