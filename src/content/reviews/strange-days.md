Strange Days is set on New Year's Eve 1999, in a Los Angeles that is one bad night away from complete dissolution. **The technology at its center — SQUID recordings, black market clips of lived experience sold in parking structures and back rooms** — is one of the most elegant cyberpunk conceits in film. It requires no explanation. Everyone understands immediately what it means to be able to replay someone else's sensory experience, and everyone understands immediately what the market for that would look like.

Lenny Nero, played by Ralph Fiennes, is a dealer in these clips. He is also, like most dealers, a dedicated user — specifically of recordings featuring his ex-girlfriend Faith, who has moved on in ways he has not. Bigelow shoots Lenny's addiction with the same visual grammar as the recordings themselves: immersive, slightly wrong, the edges of the frame unreliable.

> This is not like watching TV. This is a piece of someone's life.

James Cameron's script is pulpier than the film deserves — the conspiracy thriller elements are serviceable rather than sharp — but Bigelow elevates the material through camera work and performance. The point-of-view sequences in which SQUID recordings play are among the most technically demanding shots in the genre, achieved without digital assistance.

**Juliette Lewis as Faith and Angela Bassett as Mace carry the film's moral weight.** Strange Days arrived in 1995 with a vision of urban America under police violence that was not speculative but documentary, and the studio did not know what to do with it. The film underperformed commercially and has never fully recovered its reputation.

That reputation deserves recovery. Strange Days is the most politically alert cyberpunk film of the nineties, and Bigelow's refusal to aestheticize the violence at its center is what separates it from genre peers that are slicker but less honest.
